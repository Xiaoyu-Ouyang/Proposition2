\section{Introduction}
Quantum money originated with Wiesner's proposal of conjugate coding, where classical information is encoded into quantum states chosen from incompatible bases \cite{wiesnerConjugateCoding1983}.
In the simplest form, the bank chooses secret strings $x,\theta \in \{0,1\}^n$ and prepares
\[
|\$_s\rangle = |s\rangle \otimes \bigotimes_{i=1}^n H^{\theta_i}|x_i\rangle,
\]
where $s$ is a classical serial number and $H^{\theta_i}$ means that the $i$th qubit is prepared either in the computational basis $\{|0\rangle,|1\rangle\}$ or in the Hadamard basis $\{|+\rangle,|-\rangle\}$.
These are conjugate, or mutually unbiased, bases: measuring a qubit in the wrong basis gives a random outcome and disturbs the state.
The bank can verify the note because it stores the secret classical description $(x,\theta)$ associated with the serial number $s$, while a counterfeiter who does not know this description cannot learn the note well enough to reproduce it without disturbing it.
This was the first example of a cryptographic task whose security is genuinely quantum, since a classical string can always be copied, but an unknown quantum state cannot be cloned.
Wiesner's scheme, however, is private-key quantum money.
This means that verification requires returning the banknote to the bank, or at least interacting with an entity that knows the secret verification data.

A natural next goal is to make quantum money behave more like cash.
In a public-key quantum money scheme, anyone should be able to verify a banknote using a classical public key and a public description of a quantum verification procedure, while only the legitimate issuer should be able to mint valid notes efficiently.
Early work of Bennett, Brassard, Breidbart, and Wiesner already explored ``unforgeable subway tokens'': quantum tokens that a turnstile can verify, but that a user should not be able to copy and reuse \cite{bennettQuantumCryptographyUnforgeable1982}.
Aaronson later gave a complexity-theoretic formulation of public quantum money and proved a complexity-theoretic no-cloning theorem, giving oracle evidence that public verification need not make cloning easy \cite{aaronsonQuantumCopyProtectionQuantum2009}.
In general, the honest mint has a secret key or trapdoor and can prepare valid banknotes efficiently, while the security requirement is that a public adversary, given the public key, the public verifier, and some valid banknotes, cannot efficiently prepare or clone additional valid banknotes.

Many candidate public-key quantum money schemes can be understood by asking what the banknote state is, how public verification works, and what computational assumption prevents public state preparation.
Aaronson's early candidate used random stabilizer states and public tests, but this concrete proposal was later broken \cite{aaronsonQuantumCopyProtectionQuantum2009,lutomirskiBreakingMakingQuantum2009}.
Farhi, Gosset, Hassidim, Lutomirski, and Shor proposed money from knots, where a banknote is a superposition over equivalent knot diagrams and verification uses knot invariants \cite{farhiQuantumMoneyKnots2012}.
Its security is heuristic and topological: the intended hardness is that, given public invariants such as the Alexander polynomial, it is still hard to prepare the required superposition over equivalent diagrams.
Aaronson and Christiano proposed a hidden-subspace scheme whose verifier performs tests in complementary bases \cite{aaronsonChristianoQuantumMoneyHidden2012}.
The black-box version has strong security evidence, but making the hidden subspace publicly testable requires concrete algebraic encodings; later cryptanalysis showed that some proposed concrete hard problems for such schemes are easier than expected \cite{bilykCryptanalysisThreeQuantum2022}.
Zhandry introduced quantum lightning, a stronger ``collision-free'' primitive in which no efficient procedure should generate two valid bolts with the same serial number \cite{zhandryQuantumLightningNever2017a}.
Lightning is closely related to public-key quantum money, but it changes the minting model: generation may be public, while security forbids serial-number collisions rather than merely counterfeiting notes issued by a bank.
The mechanism is cryptographic rather than statistical: the serial number is tied to the quantum state by the verification relation, and producing two valid states with the same serial number should solve the underlying hard problem.

The search for assumptions underlying public verification has produced both constructions and barriers.
Liu, Montgomery, and Zhandry showed that a natural lattice-based route to public quantum money cannot be secure, including an attack on a scheme of Khesin, Lu, and Shor \cite{liuAnotherBreakingMaking2023}.
They also proposed invariant money, a framework abstracting the structure of knot-based money and relying on new knowledge-type assumptions; possible instantiations include group actions, rerandomizable functional encryption, isogenies, and knots.
Kane, Sharif, and Silverberg proposed a number-theoretic approach in which bills are joint eigenstates of commuting operators arising from quaternion algebras \cite{kaneQuantumMoneyQuaternion2022}.
Other works show barriers to obtaining public-key quantum money from some standard-looking tools, including collision-resistant hash functions and black-box uses of evasive obfuscation \cite{ananthImplausibilityPublicKey2023,zhandryQuantumMoneyEvasive2025}.
There is also a semi-quantum direction, initiated by Radian and Sattath, that asks how much quantumness is needed from the bank; Shmueli constructed public-key quantum money with a classical bank from quantum-secure indistinguishability obfuscation and sub-exponential LWE \cite{radianSemiQuantumMoney2019,shmueliPublicKeyQuantum2022}.
Zhandry constructed public-key quantum money and quantum lightning from abelian group actions \cite{zhandryQuantumMoneyAbelian2025}; 
Doliskani related the security of this construction in a generic group-action model to the group-action discrete logarithm problem \cite{doliskaniPublicKeyQuantum2025}; 
and Bostanci, Nehoran, and Zhandry extended the picture through a general quantum duality for group representations and a pre-action security assumption \cite{bostanciGeneralQuantumDuality2025}.

These works suggest a common verification-preparation tradeoff.
Existing public-key quantum money schemes have efficient public verification by definition, but the verifier is usually described as a public quantum circuit or an algebraic test.
This proposal asks whether the same tradeoff can be expressed in the language of Hamiltonian complexity.
Instead of viewing verification only as a public verification circuit, we ask whether the valid banknotes can be realized as low-energy states, or ground states, of a publicly specified Hamiltonian $H$.
In such a formulation, the public verifier is an energy test for $H$, while the security question becomes a ground-state preparation question: can an adversary given $H$, $\mathsf{pk}$, and $k$ valid ground states efficiently prepare more valid ground states?

The Hamiltonian viewpoint is natural because circuit verification can often be converted into local Hamiltonian constraints, and geometrically local Hamiltonians are the standard bridge between quantum computation and many-body physics \cite{gottesmanQuantumClassicalComplexity2010}.
Recent work on public-key pseudoentanglement and ground-state learning hardness also shows that cryptographic assumptions can control what can be learned or prepared from a public Hamiltonian description \cite{boulandPublickeyPseudoentanglementHardness2023,boulandHardnessLearningGround2024}.
The main question is simple: can public-key quantum money be realized as the ground space of a public local Hamiltonian?
The desired construction should keep the honest mint efficient, while making ground-state preparation hard for anyone with only public information.

\subsection{Definition}
A public-key quantum money scheme consists of an efficient minting algorithm and an efficient public verification algorithm:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathrm{Gen}(\mathsf{sk}) \to (\mathsf{pk}, |\$\rangle)$: Given a classical secret key $\mathsf{sk}$, the minting algorithm outputs a classical public key $\mathsf{pk}$ and a quantum banknote state $|\$\rangle$.
    \item $\mathrm{Ver}(|\psi\rangle,\mathsf{pk}) \to \top / \bot$: Given a candidate quantum state $|\psi\rangle$ and the classical public key $\mathsf{pk}$, the public verification algorithm accepts or rejects the state.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Security (Unclonability)}
The basic security requirement is that one valid banknote cannot be turned into two valid banknotes.
Let $\mathcal{A}$ be a quantum polynomial-time adversary that receives the public key $\mathsf{pk}$ and one valid banknote $|\$\rangle$.
The adversary applies an arbitrary quantum attack and outputs a joint state on two registers, which are intended to be two candidate banknotes:
\[
\rho_{A_1A_2} \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\mathsf{pk},|\$\rangle).
\]
Here $\rho_{A_1A_2}$ may be mixed or entangled across the two registers.
The individual candidate banknotes are the reduced states obtained by tracing out the other register:
\[
\rho_{A_1}=\operatorname{Tr}_{A_2}(\rho_{A_1A_2}), \qquad
\rho_{A_2}=\operatorname{Tr}_{A_1}(\rho_{A_1A_2}).
\]
Unclonability says that the probability that both candidate banknotes pass public verification is negligible:
\[
\Pr\!\left[
\mathrm{Ver}(\rho_{A_1},\mathsf{pk})=\top
\ \land\
\mathrm{Ver}(\rho_{A_2},\mathsf{pk})=\top
\right]
\le \operatorname{negl}(\lambda).
\]
Equivalently, no efficient adversary should be able to increase the number of valid banknotes.
The fully general definition allows an adversary to receive $k$ valid banknotes and output more than $k$ registers, and requires that the adversary's total expected number of accepting registers increases by at most a negligible amount.

\subsection{Constructions}
There are many proposed candidate constructions of public-key quantum money.
For this proposal, two representative families are especially relevant: constructions based on post-quantum indistinguishability obfuscation, and constructions based on cryptographic group actions.
Both families try to solve the same basic problem: publish enough information to verify a state, while hiding enough structure so that the public information does not become a state-preparation algorithm.

\paragraph{Post-quantum indistinguishability obfuscation.}
Indistinguishability obfuscation (iO) is a way to publish a circuit while hiding how the circuit works.
Informally, if two circuits compute the same function, then their obfuscations should be computationally indistinguishable:
\[
C_0(x)=C_1(x)\ \text{for all }x
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\mathsf{iO}(C_0)\approx_{\mathrm{q}}\mathsf{iO}(C_1),
\]
where $\approx_{\mathrm{q}}$ means indistinguishability for efficient quantum adversaries.
The iO-based public-key quantum money route is most cleanly understood through the hidden-subspace scheme of Aaronson and Christiano and Zhandry's subspace-hiding obfuscation approach \cite{aaronsonChristianoQuantumMoneyHidden2012,zhandryQuantumLightningNever2017a}.
The mint samples a hidden subspace $S\subseteq \mathbb{F}^n$ and prepares the uniform superposition
\[
|\$_S\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt{|S|}}\sum_{x\in S}|x\rangle.
\]
The Fourier transform of $|\$_S\rangle$ is the uniform superposition over the dual subspace $S^\perp$.
Thus verification can test membership in $S$, apply the Fourier transform, and then test membership in $S^\perp$.
To make this verification public without revealing $S$, the public key contains obfuscated membership programs
\[
\mathsf{pk}=(\widehat{\mathbf{1}}_S,\widehat{\mathbf{1}}_{S^\perp}),
\qquad
\widehat{\mathbf{1}}_S\approx \mathsf{iO}(\mathbf{1}_S),\quad
\widehat{\mathbf{1}}_{S^\perp}\approx \mathsf{iO}(\mathbf{1}_{S^\perp}).
\]
Here the security intuition is not that iO hides an arbitrary verifier circuit, but that the obfuscated membership tests hide enough information about the subspace to prevent preparing an additional copy of $|\$_S\rangle$.
Shmueli's public-key semi-quantum money construction uses a related subspace-hiding idea, but in a different model: a classical bank interacts with a quantum receiver, and security relies on quantum-secure iO together with sub-exponential LWE \cite{shmueliPublicKeyQuantum2022}.

\paragraph{Group actions.}
The group-action approach gives a more direct bridge to projectors and Hamiltonians.
In Zhandry's abelian construction, an abelian group $G$ acts on a finite set $X$, written $g\star x$.
For a starting element $x\in X$, the mint creates a superposition over group actions and then Fourier samples a group label $h$.
The resulting serial number is $h$, and the banknote has the form
\[
|G_h\star x\rangle
=\frac{1}{\sqrt{|G|}}\sum_{g\in G}\chi(g,h)\ket{g\star x},
\]
where $\chi(g,h)$ is the relevant character of the abelian group \cite{zhandryQuantumMoneyAbelian2025}.
Public verification applies the group action in superposition and uses a quantum Fourier transform to check that the measured label is the claimed serial number $h$.
The security of this abelian construction is proved in the generic group-action model under strengthened group-action hardness assumptions, and Doliskani gives a related generic-model standard-assumption direction \cite{doliskaniPublicKeyQuantum2025}.

Bostanci, Nehoran, and Zhandry generalize the picture to non-Abelian group actions and express verification representation-theoretically \cite{bostanciGeneralQuantumDuality2025}.
The action defines a unitary representation on the Hilbert space spanned by $\{\ket{x}:x\in X\}$:
\[
R(g)=\sum_{x\in X}\ket{g\star x}\bra{x}.
\]
For an irreducible representation $\lambda$ of $G$, a minted money state has the form
\[
|\$_{\lambda,i,j}\rangle
=\sqrt{\frac{d_\lambda}{|G|}}
\sum_{g\in G}\rho_\lambda(g^{-1})_{i,j}\ket{g\star x}.
\]
Verification checks whether the state lies in the corresponding Fourier or isotypic subspace.
At the level of projectors, the verifier can be summarized by the Fourier-isotypic projector
\[
P_\lambda=\frac{d_\lambda}{|G|}
\sum_{g\in G}\chi_\lambda(g)^*R(g).
\]
Here $d_\lambda$ is the dimension of $\lambda$ and $\chi_\lambda$ is its character.
The serial number is the irrep label $\lambda$, and a valid banknote lies in the corresponding accepting subspace.
This gives a non-local Hamiltonian whose ground space is the valid money subspace:
\[
H_\lambda=I-P_\lambda,
\qquad
\operatorname{Tr}(P_\lambda\rho)\approx 1
\ \Longleftrightarrow\
\rho \text{ has low energy with respect to } H_\lambda.
\]
In the non-Abelian setting, the relevant plain-model security assumption is pre-action security; this assumption only becomes meaningful once actions and pre-actions differ, which is why the non-Abelian generalization is important.

\paragraph{Circuit-to-Hamiltonian Transformation:}
The circuit-to-Hamiltonian statement should be interpreted in the Feynman-Kitaev history-state sense \cite{aharonovAdiabaticQuantumComputation2008}.
Given a circuit $C=U_T\cdots U_1$ and input $|0\rangle$, one can construct a local Hamiltonian $H_C$ whose ground state encodes the whole computation:
\[
|\Psi_{\mathrm{hist}}\rangle
=\frac{1}{\sqrt{T+1}}\sum_{t=0}^T
\left(U_t\cdots U_1|0\rangle\right)\otimes |t\rangle_{\mathrm{clock}}.
\]
Here the $t=0$ term is interpreted as the input state $|0\rangle$.
The output $C|0\rangle$ appears in the final clock sector, not usually as the entire ground state by itself.
Thus the main Hamiltonian question is whether the verification projector, or an equivalent verifier, can be made local while keeping the valid money state recoverable from the resulting ground space.

\paragraph{Open Question:}
The group-action construction is a natural starting point for the Hamiltonian formulation because its verifier already has a projector form: valid money states lie in a Fourier or isotypic subspace, giving the non-local Hamiltonian $H_\lambda=I-P_\lambda$.
The remaining question is whether this non-local verification Hamiltonian can be replaced by a local, preferably \textbf{1D/2D geometrically local}, Hamiltonian without losing the cryptographic properties.
Gottesman and Irani show that low-dimensional Hamiltonian systems can encode highly nontrivial computation through quantum Turing-machine-to-Hamiltonian techniques \cite{gottesmanQuantumClassicalComplexity2010}.
More recent work on public-key pseudoentanglement and ground-state learning hardness shows how public-key cryptographic state constructions can be translated into Hamiltonian hardness, first for general local Hamiltonians and then for geometrically local Hamiltonians using related ideas \cite{boulandPublickeyPseudoentanglementHardness2023,boulandHardnessLearningGround2024}.
This motivates the central open question: can a public-key quantum money construction, especially a group-action based construction, be realized as a public geometrically local Hamiltonian whose ground states remain efficiently mintable by the issuer but hard to prepare or clone from public information?

\section{Complexity-Theoretic Motivation: QMA and Clonable QMA}

The Hamiltonian formulation naturally connects public-key quantum money to QMA. In QMA, a polynomial-time quantum verifier receives a polynomial-size quantum witness. Completeness says that for every YES instance $x$, there exists a witness $|\psi\rangle$ accepted with high probability; soundness says that for every NO instance, no quantum witness is accepted with high probability \cite{aharonovQuantumNPSurvey2002}.

\subsection{Local Hamiltonian as a QMA Witness Problem}
The local Hamiltonian problem is the canonical QMA-complete problem. Given a $k$-local Hamiltonian
\[
H=\sum_i H_i,
\]
one must distinguish the case $\lambda_{\min}(H)\le a$ from the case $\lambda_{\min}(H)\ge b$, promised that $b-a\ge 1/\operatorname{poly}(n)$. In a YES instance, a low-energy state, in particular a ground state, serves as the QMA witness. Kitaev's construction established QMA-completeness of local Hamiltonian, and Kempe, Kitaev, and Regev later showed that even the 2-local Hamiltonian problem is QMA-complete \cite{aharonovQuantumNPSurvey2002,kempeComplexityLocalHamiltonian2006}.

For this proposal, the relevant point is not only that ground states can be verified as QMA witnesses, but that they may be hard to reproduce. The target question is therefore: can a publicly specified local Hamiltonian have efficiently verifiable ground states that remain hard to prepare or clone from public information?

\subsection{Clonable QMA}
Nehoran and Zhandry introduced $\mathsf{clonableQMA}$ to formalize QMA problems whose valid quantum witnesses are efficiently clonable \cite{nehoranComputationalSeparationQuantum2024}. Informally, a language is in $\mathsf{clonableQMA}$ if there are a QMA verifier $V$ and an efficient quantum cloner $C$ such that, for every YES instance $x$, there exists a witness $|\psi_x\rangle$ that both makes $V$ accept and can be cloned by $C$ with high fidelity:
\[
C(|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|)\approx |\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\otimes |\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|.
\]
They show the containment
\[
\mathsf{QCMA}\subseteq \mathsf{clonableQMA}\subseteq \mathsf{QMA},
\]
where $\mathsf{QCMA}$ has classical witnesses and $\mathsf{QMA}$ allows arbitrary quantum witnesses. This makes $\mathsf{clonableQMA}$ the right complexity-theoretic language for separating reusable, efficiently copyable witnesses from genuinely quantum witnesses.

\subsection{Evidence from Spectral Forrelation}
Bostanci, Haferkamp, Nirkhe, and Zhandry give a classical-oracle separation between $\mathsf{QMA}$ and $\mathsf{QCMA}$ using spectral Forrelation \cite{bostanciSeparatingQMAQCMA2025}. For subsets $S,U\subseteq \{0,1\}^n$, let
\[
\Pi_S=\sum_{x\in S}|x\rangle\langle x|,
\qquad
\Pi_U=\sum_{x\in U}|x\rangle\langle x|.
\]
The spectral Forrelation value is
\[
\|\Pi_U \mathsf{Had}^{\otimes n}\Pi_S\|_{\mathrm{op}}^2
=
\max_{\|\psi\|=1}\|\Pi_U \mathsf{Had}^{\otimes n}\Pi_S|\psi\rangle\|^2.
\]
The QMA witness is the top singular vector for this operator. The key intuition is that a classical witness can be reused to generate many samples, whereas a quantum witness is a use-once object. This supports the proposal's main viewpoint: unclonability can be a computational resource, and Hamiltonian ground states are a natural place to ask for publicly verifiable but non-reproducible quantum witnesses.

\section{Group Actions and Oracles}

The group-action construction suggests a concrete route toward Hamiltonian quantum money.
Its public verifier is already a representation-theoretic test: for a claimed serial number, valid banknotes lie in a Fourier or isotypic subspace.
Equivalently, the verifier defines a public, generally non-local, accepting projector $P_\lambda$ and hence a formal Hamiltonian $H_\lambda=I-P_\lambda$ whose ground space is the valid money subspace \cite{zhandryQuantumMoneyAbelian2025,bostanciGeneralQuantumDuality2025}.

The main difficulty is to replace this non-local verification Hamiltonian by a local, preferably geometrically local, Hamiltonian without making the state efficiently preparable from public information.
One possible approach is not to localize $P_\lambda$ directly, but to start from the public verification circuit and apply a circuit-to-Hamiltonian construction.
The resulting ground state would usually be a history state rather than the original banknote, so one would need a reduction showing that preparing or cloning low-energy states yields a counterfeiting attack against the original group-action money scheme \cite{aharonovAdiabaticQuantumComputation2008}.
The work on public-key pseudoentanglement and ground-state learning hardness gives evidence that public-key cryptographic hardness can survive Hamiltonianization, and that more geometric versions may be reachable using QTM-to-Hamiltonian or related low-dimensional constructions \cite{gottesmanQuantumClassicalComplexity2010,boulandPublickeyPseudoentanglementHardness2023,boulandHardnessLearningGround2024}.

For the non-Abelian group-action route, the relevant security assumption is pre-action security.
Let $G$ act on $X$, written $g\star x$.
Relative to a fixed starting element $x$, an ordinary action maps $g\star x$ to $(h_1g)\star x$, while a pre-action by $h_2$ maps it roughly to $(gh_2^{-1})\star x$.
A bi-action combines the two, sending
\[
g\star x \longmapsto (h_1gh_2^{-1})\star x.
\]
Pre-action indistinguishability asks that no efficient quantum adversary can distinguish a random action from a random bi-action.
This assumption is meaningful mainly in the non-Abelian setting, since for abelian group actions the action and pre-action essentially coincide \cite{bostanciGeneralQuantumDuality2025}.
